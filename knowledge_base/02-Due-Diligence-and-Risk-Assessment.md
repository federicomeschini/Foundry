# 02. Due Diligence: The Investigative Filter for Risk

## 1. Introduction to the Investigative Filter

Due Diligence (DD) is the clinical process of de-risking an investment by identifying, quantifying, and mitigating information asymmetry. In the venture ecosystem, DD is not a "check-the-box" exercise; it is an adversarial-yet-constructive stress test of a startup's entire existence. This manual serves as the blueprint for an automated DD tool, shifting the focus from the founder's narrative to empirical reality. We move from "what is said" to "what can be proven."

The goal of this investigative filter is to determine the probability of a venture's success by analyzing its core pillars: Team, Business, Market, Technology, and Financials. Every question listed below is a diagnostic probe designed to reveal structural integrity or hidden rot.

---

## 2. Pillar 1: Management Team

The management team is the single most critical variable in early-stage venture success. We investigate the founders not just as creators, but as operators and fiduciaries.

### Investigative Questions:

1.  **"What does your gut say about the people? Do you think that you could get on with the entrepreneur in a professional capacity – it can be a long-term relationship so it’s good to have mutual trust. But more than that, do you think other people will trust them?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Gut feeling is the human brain's pattern-matching engine for integrity. "Trustworthiness" is a proxy for the ability to hire talent and raise follow-on capital. If an investor cannot trust a founder, neither will a Tier-1 VC or a critical executive hire.

2.  **"Carry out background checks on the entrepreneur - take personal references, credit checks, Company House records. Check Google, Facebook and LinkedIn for shared connections and previous experience."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Past behavior is the best predictor of future performance. Negative signals in Company House (e.g., failed ventures with suspicious circumstances) or poor credit indicate a lack of fiscal discipline or integrity.

3.  **"Do you think they understand their business? How well do they explain it to you? Do they understand and are they realistic about their weaknesses and obstacles?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Intellectual honesty is non-negotiable. A founder who glosses over weaknesses is dangerous. We look for the "curse of knowledge"—the ability to simplify complex concepts—as a marker of true mastery.

4.  **"Are they realistic about their own personal limitations? Do they know what skills they need to add to complete the team? Do they have the management skills to market the business in its early stages? Do they have the skillset to collect the right data and manage their financials?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Self-awareness prevents "founder-led bottlenecking." We assess whether the founder is a "specialist" who can delegate or a "generalist" who will eventually be overwhelmed.

5.  **"Do they have a realistic exit plan? What’s the long-term vision for the business and their role within it?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Investors need liquidity. If the founder views the company as a "forever lifestyle business," the incentives are misaligned. We seek "exit-oriented" builders.

6.  **"How committed are they? Can they support themselves through the early stages? How desperate are they? Can they afford to lose? Do they have personal financial commitments that may make taking risks harder e.g. mortgages?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** The "Commitment-Desperation Ratio." We want high commitment but not desperation. Desperate founders make short-term, survival-based decisions that destroy long-term equity value.

7.  **"How professional is their business plan? Did they do it themselves or with professional help? If outsourced, was it from laziness/incompetence or to get quality input? Is the market research geared to suit their narrative or substantiated in real data?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** The business plan is a proxy for the founder's rigorousness. Outsourced plans often lack the "founder's DNA," leading to operational disconnects.

8.  **"How flexible are they on shareholder control? If they are determined to maintain executive control, do they have the competence to deserve it?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Governance risk. If a founder demands control without the competence to execute, the board's ability to correct a failing course is neutralized.

9.  **"How well do the founders know each other? Have they worked together before? Do you think they can survive and thrive under the pressures of running a company?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Co-founder friction is a top-3 cause of startup death. Shared history (the "battle-tested" factor) significantly reduces the probability of a catastrophic fallout.

10. **"You will have your own way of gauging people, but the UKBAA recommends focussing on the three Ps: People, Passion and Perseverance."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** While "Passion" is subjective, it translates to "Resilience." We look for founders who view obstacles as problems to be solved, not reasons to quit.

## 3. Pillar 2: The Business

This pillar assesses the structural health of the entity and the alignment of the investment terms.

### Investigative Questions:

1.  **"You should review the shares already issued – request to see how the current ownership of the business looks according to class of share. This will normally be displayed in a Share Capitalisation or Caps Table. You will probably be investing in ordinary shares (for tax reasons) – you should check that there aren’t other share classes with preferential rights which could dilute your value. You can use the Caps Table to acquaint yourself with the other shareholders. In early rounds, you should make sure all shares are actually paid up before you commit."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Cap Table cleanliness is vital. "Ghost shareholders" or complex preferential rights can make a company "uninvestable" for later-stage VCs. Unpaid shares represent a legal and financial liability.

2.  **"Evaluate the funding they are seeking. Do they have a realistic strategy for achieving it? What do they need the funds for? Are they asking for too little to accomplish this? Are they asking for too much (which will reduce your upside)? Do they envisage future funding rounds? And will this round allow them to accomplish what they need to in order to raise another round at a higher valuation?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Capital allocation efficiency. "Asking for too little" leads to premature cash-outs; "asking for too much" creates unrealistic valuation hurdles for the next round.

3.  **"Examine the share price. What does the share price mean for the overall value of the company? How did they decide on the share price?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Valuation logic. We look for "Comparable Company Analysis" or "First Chicago Method" reasoning. Arbitrary pricing suggests a lack of financial sophistication.

4.  **"Make sure you understand what their valuation is. It can become confusing in decks and business plans. The ‘post-money’ valuation will determine the proportion of the company you end up owning."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Mathematical clarity. Misunderstanding the difference between pre-money and post-money leads to "unintentional dilution" and ruined investor relations.

5.  **"Get to grips with what the business owns. Early-stage companies are unlikely to have many tangible assets, so the intangible value adds will be very important to justify their valuation. Look for registered IP rights as well as unregistered things like contact and letters of intent. If the company is claiming anything ask for proof."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Asset verification. In the absence of revenue, the "Moat" is the value. LOIs (Letters of Intent) are proxies for market validation.

6.  **"Evaluate the effect of the overall ownership following the investment round. Will the day-to-day team be sufficiently incentivised to work hard towards an exit? Watch out for situations where the management team ends up with a small stake or where there are loads of small shareholders."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Incentive alignment. If founders are diluted below 15-20% too early, they lose the "owner's mindset" and become "employees," significantly increasing the risk of abandonment.

7.  **"What’s the potential exit value? Does the team have a reasonable exit prediction and strategy to achieve it? Will an exit give you sufficient ROI to justify the risk? This can be very difficult to determine accurately but discussing it with the businesses founders will give you a good idea of their competence."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Reverse-engineering the outcome. We look for "Exit Multiples" in the specific sector. A founder who doesn't know who their likely acquirers are hasn't thought through the endgame.

8.  **"What professional advice has the company received e.g. accounting, legal, IP? Is it good advice from reputed sources? Ultimately, do you think the directors have a solid grasp of the risks; and do they have ideas in place to overcome them?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Governance maturity. High-quality advisors (Big 4, Magic Circle, etc.) act as early-stage gatekeepers of quality. "Cheap" advice often leads to "expensive" legal cleanups later.

9.  **"Try to verify and authentic everything you can. Use trusted third party sources to get information on the company’s accounts and its directors. You can pay companies to check all this for you, but the cost should be proportionate to the deal and the information you need should be publicly available."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** The "Trust but Verify" principle. Third-party verification removes founder bias.

10. **"Check whether there are any tax breaks available in your region for investors investing in early stage ventures. If there are, check that the company has registered for them if relevant. Tax breaks can seriously reduce the amount of capital you actually risk when making an investment of this sort."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Risk-adjusted return optimization. Schemes like SEIS/EIS in the UK provide 30-50% downside protection through tax relief, fundamentally changing the IRR (Internal Rate of Return) profile.

## 4. Pillar 3: The Market

A great team with a great product in a bad market will fail. We analyze the "Market Reality."

### Investigative Questions:

1.  **"Is the company clear on who their customers and users are and why they will want the product/service? It’s important to realise that customers and users may not be the same people, but the company will need to satisfy both."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Customer-User dichotomy. In B2B SaaS, the *user* wants efficiency, but the *customer* (buyer) wants ROI. Failure to satisfy both leads to high churn or low adoption.

2.  **"Who is going to pay for the product/service? This may be the customer or user but could be another party entirely. Either way, are they likely to pay for it?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Monetization logic. "Free users" do not equal "Revenue." We look for a clear path to "Willingness to Pay" (WTP).

3.  **"What are the USPs (unique selling points? i.e. Why would someone choose this product/service over competitors?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** The Moat. A 10% improvement is not enough; we look for a "10x better" solution to overcome the high switching costs of incumbents.

4.  **"How large is the demand/need for this product/service? Is the demand likely to grow in the future? You should check any figures given by the entrepreneur about the market size with trusted sources."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** TAM/SAM/SOM verification. Founders often inflate TAM by including irrelevant sectors. We look for "Serviceable Obtainable Market" (SOM) realism.

5.  **"Who are the competitors? Gather as much information as possible to evaluate whether the idea is strong enough to beat the competition and succeed."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Competitive landscape. "No competitors" usually means "No market." We assess "Direct," "Indirect," and "Status Quo" competition.

6.  **"Get to grips with the dynamics of their sales channel. Do they sell to customers or to businesses? Or perhaps through a distributor? The nature of their sales channel will have implications on their cost of sales and the difficulty of their marketing."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Unit Economics. Sales channels dictate CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost). A B2C app requiring a direct sales force is a structural failure.

7.  **"Has the company identified where it is going to sell its product? Do you think this initial market is big enough, suitable enough and accessible enough?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Beachhead strategy. Success in a "niche" (e.g., Salesforce starting with CRM) is the only way to eventually dominate the broad market.

8.  **"What is the time and cost to market? This will help you gauge how much money the sales need to make to turn a profit. And how likely is it that competitors could do something similar in the same period and for a similar cost?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Speed-to-Market vs. Burn. If the "Time to Market" exceeds the "Cash Runway," the company is dead on arrival.

9.  **"Is the selling price justified? Are customers happy with the price? What’s the elasticity? How does the price compare with competitor products/services?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Pricing power. High elasticity (price sensitivity) indicates a "commodity" product with no brand moat.

10. **"Is the market established, emerging or is the company aiming to create a new market? If the former two, then the customers will be educated to some degree about the product/service? If the latter, then the company will need to persuade and educate customers about the product/service – do they have a convincing to do this?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Education Cost Risk. "Market Creation" is the most expensive path. We assess if the company has the "Narrative Power" and capital to survive the long education cycle.

## 5. Pillar 4: Technology

Technology must be more than "functional"; it must be "defensible" and "scalable."

### Investigative Questions:

1.  **"Is the technology standalone or does it rely on integration with other businesses/technologies? Are the businesses it relies on customers? And if so, can the business prove the demand from them?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Platform Risk. Dependency on a single API (e.g., OpenAI or Twitter) creates a "Terminal Flaw" if the host platform changes terms.

2.  **"Does the technology have many potential applications (platform technology)? If so, has the business worked out and justified an initial focus?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Focused Execution. "Platform plays" often fail because they try to be everything to everyone. We look for "High-Conviction initial use-cases."

3.  **"Is it or can it be protected by IP rights? How and where?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Defensibility. Patents are defensive; "Trade Secrets" are operational. We assess whether the tech can be "reverse-engineered" in 6 months by a well-funded competitor.

4.  **"Is the technology novel and disruptive or an improvement on existing tech? For disruptive tech, you will want to see proof of its efficacy and justification for the potential market adoption. For an improved tech, there is potentially reduced risk because the market is established, but it is enough of an improvement to be a success?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Innovation Risk. Incrementalism is rarely venture-scale. We look for "Step-change" improvements.

5.  **"Is there anything else like it? What’s different about it? Are there any concerns over potential IP and product disputes with other companies?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** IP Litigation Risk. A great product with a "Freedom to Operate" (FTO) issue is a liability, not an asset.

6.  **"Is it launched and in the marketplace? What has the response and feedback been so far?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Product-Market Fit (PMF) indicators. Beta feedback is the "Truth" that contradicts the pitch deck.

7.  **"Is it ready for market? If not, what obstacles does it face and is the strategy for overcoming them realistic?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** R&D Risk. "Vaporware" risk is high in deep-tech. We look for a "Minimum Viable Product" (MVP) that actually solves a core problem.

8.  **"Who are the current big players in this space and what are they currently working on? If they are working on something similar and they are established members of an existing supply chain, it may be difficult for new solutions to compete."**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Incumbent Response Risk. If Microsoft or Google can add this feature in a weekend, the startup has no future.

9.  **"How quickly are the technology trends changing? Can this idea gain a foothold in the market before even newer technology overtakes it?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Obsolescence Risk. In AI/Crypto, the cycle is 3-6 months. The tech must be "Future-proof" or "Agile" enough to pivot.

## 6. Pillar 5: Financials

Financials are the "Scorecard of Reality." Projections are fiction; the Balance Sheet is fact.

### Investigative Questions:

1.  **"Does the company have detailed and well-substantiated projections?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Model Integrity. We look for "Bottom-up" projections (based on sales activity) rather than "Top-down" (% of market).

2.  **"Has the company used an independent accounting firm to prepare and audit its financials?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Verification. Self-prepared accounts often hide "Creative Accounting" or misclassified expenses.

3.  **"Has the company made sound assumptions in preparing its projections?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Sensitivity Analysis. We test the "Levers"—if a 10% increase in CAC kills the business, the assumptions are too fragile.

4.  **"Are the expected revenues realistic? Are they supported by evidence from existing orders?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Pipeline Quality. We distinguish between "Soft interest" and "Binding contracts."

5.  **"How long before the company is cash-flow positive and what cash requirements are needed to get it there?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** The "Default Alive" vs. "Default Dead" test. We calculate the "Capital Infusion" required to reach self-sustainability.

6.  **"Has the company raised money previously? If so, how much was raised and what were the terms?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Signaling Risk. If previous investors aren't participating (follow-on), it's a massive "Red Flag."

7.  **"How much funding, if any, will the company need in future? Has the company thought about its future needs properly?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Dilution Roadmap. If the company needs $100M to reach $10M ARR, the capital intensity is too high for a standard VC model.

8.  **"What are the on-going financial commitments of the business (leases, debt repayments, salary costs)? How far can the overheads be varied to support the business in case of a slump in performance?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Fixed-Cost Rigidity. High fixed costs (OpEx) during a pivot lead to bankruptcy. We look for "Variable Cost" flexibility.

9.  **"Is the company’s balance sheet in good order? Are creditors being paid on time and is every liability accounted for?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Hidden Liabilities. Unpaid VAT or payroll taxes are "Priority Claims" that can wipe out equity holders in a liquidation.

10. **"What is the value of the assets given on the balance sheet? Are these valuations reasonable?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Asset Impairment. Overvalued IP or "Goodwill" on the balance sheet is a sign of financial mismanagement.

11. **"What’s the company’s exit strategy? How far does this depend on factors beyond the management’s control?"**
    *   **Technical Rationale:** Strategic Optionality. We prefer companies with "Multiple Exit Paths" (M&A, IPO, Secondary) over those that *must* IPO to succeed.

## 7. Section 6: Tax Specific Questions (UK Only)

In the UK context, tax compliance is a proxy for operational hygiene and a source of significant investor protection.

1.  **Is the company eligible for SEIS and/or EIS tax relief?** (Critical for Angel/Seed risk mitigation).
2.  **Have all taxes been paid on time?** (Tests fiscal responsibility).
3.  **Have all tax returns been filed on time?** (Indicators of administrative competence).
4.  **Is the company eligible for R&D relief and has a claim been made?** (Crucial non-dilutive funding source).
5.  **Has the company ever been investigated by HMRC?** (Red flag for systemic risk).
6.  **Are all staff paid through the payroll? Are there any freelancer and consultants who should be treated as an employee?** (Mitigates IR35 and employment law liabilities).
7.  **Is the Company VAT registered? Who is responsible for the tax compliance function?**
8.  **Who is responsible for the tax compliance function?** (Note: Verification of internal controls).
9.  **Does the Company have any tax losses carried forward, and if so what are the nature of these losses (e.g., trading or non-trading/capital)?** (Tax assets can improve net exit value).
10. **Has the Company undertaken any tax planning or claimed special reliefs from taxation?** (Assesses sophistication and exposure).

## 8. Section 7: Decision Framework (Fixable vs. Terminal)

Once the data is collected, the VC Lead must categorize findings into two categories:

### A. Fixable Gaps (Investment can proceed with conditions)
*   **Missing C-Suite Roles:** (Resolved by hiring plan).
*   **Messy Cap Table:** (Resolved by "Cleanup" clauses in the Term Sheet).
*   **Sub-optimal Pricing:** (Resolved by post-investment board guidance).
*   **Lack of R&D Tax Claim:** (Easily corrected by professional tax advisors).

### B. Terminal Flaws (Automatic Rejection)
*   **Integrity Failure:** Any discrepancy in background checks or audited financials.
*   **Founder Inflexibility:** Resistance to shareholder control or professional advice during DD.
*   **Platform Dependency:** No clear path to surviving a host platform's API change.
*   **Negative Unit Economics:** A business model that loses more money as it scales (unfixable burn).
*   **Cap Table Ruin:** Founders diluted below 10% before Series A.

**Conclusion:** The DD process is designed to find reasons to say "No." Only by failing to find a "Terminal Flaw" through this exhaustive probe do we earn the right to say "Yes."
