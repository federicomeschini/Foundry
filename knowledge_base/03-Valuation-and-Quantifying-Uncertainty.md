# 03. Valuation Methodologies: Quantifying Uncertainty

This manual provides the technical specifications, mathematical formulas, and logical frameworks required to build a robust valuation engine for venture-stage investments. It covers methods ranging from relative multiples to advanced risk-quantified discounted cash flows.

---

## 1. Relative Valuation: Multiples-Based Framework

Relative valuation is the primary tool for "sanity checks" and terminal value estimation. In the venture context, where earnings are often negative, asset-specific and top-line multiples are prioritized.

### 1.1 Key Performance Indicators (KPIs) and Definitions
To build a valuation engine, the following inputs must be standardized:

*   **MAU (Monthly Active Users):** The number of unique users who perform at least one high-value action within a 30-day window.
    *   *Logic:* Used for social platforms and non-monetized consumer tech.
*   **MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue):** The predictable revenue generated by customers per month.
    *   *Formula:* $MRR = \sum (\text{Monthly Subscription Fees}) + (\text{Annual Fees} / 12)$
*   **ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue):** The annualized version of MRR.
    *   *Formula:* $ARR = MRR \times 12$
*   **MUV (Monthly Unique Visitors):** The total count of unique individuals visiting a web property.
*   **ARPU (Average Revenue Per User):**
    *   *Formula:* $ARPU = \frac{\text{Total Revenue}}{\text{Total Active Users}}$
*   **ARPA (Average Revenue Per Account):**
    *   *Formula:* $ARPA = \frac{\text{Total Revenue}}{\text{Total Unique Accounts}}$
*   **Customer Conversion Rate (CCR):**
    *   *Formula:* $CCR = \frac{\text{Signups or Paying Customers per Month}}{\text{MUV per Month}}$

### 1.2 Multiples Selection Logic
The valuation engine should select multiples based on the startup's maturity and financial state:

| Financial State | Recommended Multiple | Rationale |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Pre-Revenue / Early Traction** | **EV/Users (MAU)** or **EV/MUV** | Focus on network effects and growth potential before monetization. |
| **High Growth / Scaling** | **EV/Sales (Revenue)** | Revenue growth is the primary driver of value; margins are secondary. |
| **Mature / Unit-Profitable** | **EV/EBITDA** | Focus on operating cash flow and efficiency. |
| **Public Comparable-Ready** | **P/E (Price-to-Earnings)** | Used only when the company has consistent net earnings. |

**Selection Logic for Multiples:**
*   **P/E vs. EBITDA:** Use EBITDA when the company is profitable at the operating level but has high non-cash expenses (depreciation/amortization) or complex debt structures. Use P/E only for companies with stable, predictable net income.
*   **Sales Multiples:** The "Standard Bearer" for SaaS. The engine should adjust for growth rates using the **Rule of 40** (Growth Rate + Profit Margin should be > 40%).

## 2. Market Sizing and Adoption Logic

### 2.1 TAM / SAM / SOM Framework
Revenue forecasting must be grounded in market reality rather than "top-down" percentage grabs.

*   **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** The global demand for a product/service category if it had 100% market share.
*   **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** The segment of TAM targeted by the startup's products/services within its geographical and technological reach.
*   **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** The portion of SAM the company can realistically capture in the short-to-medium term.

**Revenue Forecasting Logic:**
$$Revenue_t = SOM_t \times \text{Market Share}_t \times \text{Price}_t$$
Where $Market Share_t$ is derived from the adoption curve.

### 2.2 The Bass Diffusion Model
For consumer products, the adoption curve is modeled using the Bass Formula to identify comparables at the adoption level.

**The Formula:**
$$f(t) = \left[ p + q \frac{F(t)}{M} \right] \left[ M - F(t) \right]$$
*   $f(t)$: Number of new adopters at time $t$.
*   $F(t)$: Cumulative number of adopters by time $t$.
*   $M$: Market potential (total number of eventual adopters).
*   $p$: **Coefficient of Innovation** (external influence, typically 0.01 - 0.03).
*   $q$: **Coefficient of Imitation** (internal influence/word of mouth, typically 0.3 - 0.5).

**Logic for Engine Implementation:**
1.  **Parameter Estimation:** The engine should hold a database of $p$ and $q$ values for different industries (e.g., $q$ for social media is much higher than for enterprise software).
2.  **S-Curve Generation:** By integrating the formula, the engine generates the cumulative adoption curve $F(t) = M \frac{1 - e^{-(p+q)t}}{1 + \frac{q}{p}e^{-(p+q)t}}$.

## 3. Discounted Cash Flow (DCF): Advanced Methods

Startups require specialized DCF approaches due to the extreme risk profile and the binary nature of success.

### 3.1 RADR Method (Risk-Adjusted Discount Rate)
The RADR method applies a single, very high discount rate to "expected" cash flows.

**Logic:**
*   **Discount Rate ($r$):** 40% - 70% (reflecting the probability of failure and illiquidity).
*   **Formula:**
    $$PV = \sum_{t=1}^{n} \frac{E(CF_t)}{(1+r)^t}$$
*   **Application:** Used for early-stage ventures where calculating a precise Beta is impossible. The high $r$ acts as a "hurdle" that compensates for the risk of total loss.

### 3.2 CEQ Method (Certainty Equivalent) - Technical Specification
The CEQ method separates time-value-of-money from risk-adjustment. It is the preferred method for complex valuations where risk changes over time.

**The Exact Formula:**
$$PV = \frac{C_t}{(1+r)^t} = \frac{CEQ_t}{(1+r_f)^t}$$

**Certainty Equivalent ($CEQ_t$) Calculation Logic:**
As derived from the Z456 Valuation Template, the $CEQ_t$ is the "risky" expected cash flow adjusted downward by a risk penalty based on market correlation.

$$CEQ_t = E(CF_t) - \text{Risk Adjustment}$$
$$\text{Risk Adjustment} = \rho \cdot \sigma(CF_t) \cdot \frac{r_m - r_f}{\sigma_m}$$

*   $E(CF_t)$: Expected cash flow (probability-weighted).
*   $\rho$: Correlation between the project's cash flows and the market (Project-Market Correlation).
*   $\sigma(CF_t)$: Standard deviation of the project's cash flows.
*   $r_m - r_f$: Market Risk Premium.
*   $\sigma_m$: Market Standard Deviation.
*   $r_f$: Risk-free rate.

**Engine Implementation Steps:**
1.  Calculate $E(CF_t)$ and $\sigma(CF_t)$ from discrete scenarios (Success, Likely, Failure).
2.  Input Market Sharpe Ratio $(\frac{r_m - r_f}{\sigma_m})$ and Project-Market Correlation $(\rho)$.
3.  Compute $CEQ_t$ for each year.
4.  Discount $CEQ_t$ using only the risk-free rate $r_f$.

## 4. The VC Method (Sahlman)

The VC Method is the industry standard for determining ownership requirements in a financing round.

### 4.1 Step-by-Step Mathematical Workflow
1.  **Estimate Investment Needed ($I$):** Capital required for the current round.
2.  **Forecast Exit Value ($V_{\text{exit}}$):** The enterprise value at the time of IPO/M&A (Year $T$).
    *   $V_{\text{exit}} = \text{Terminal Revenue} \times \text{Exit Multiple}$.
3.  **Calculate Target Value ($V_{\text{target}}$):** The value the investment must grow to to meet the VC's required IRR.
    *   $V_{\text{target}} = I \times (1 + IRR)^T$
4.  **Required Ownership at Exit ($P_{\text{exit}}$):**
    $$P_{\text{exit}} = \frac{V_{\text{target}}}{V_{\text{exit}}}$$
5.  **Adjust for Anticipated Dilution ($D$):**
    If the company needs future rounds (Series B, C), the current investor's stake will be diluted.
    *   **Anticipated Dilution ($D$):** Estimated percentage of equity given away in future rounds.
    *   **Required Ownership Today ($P_{\text{today}}$):**
        $$P_{\text{today}} = \frac{P_{\text{exit}}}{1 - D}$$

### 4.2 Numerical Example for Engine Validation
*   **Investment:** $5M
*   **Exit Year:** 5
*   **Target IRR:** 50%
*   **Forecasted Exit Value:** $100M
*   **Future Dilution:** 25%

**Calculation:**
1.  $V_{\text{target}} = 5 \times (1 + 0.50)^5 = 5 \times 7.59375 = $37.97M
2.  $P_{\text{exit}} = 37.97 / 100 = 37.97\%$
3.  $P_{\text{today}} = 37.97\% / (1 - 0.25) = 50.62\%$
4.  **Post-Money Valuation:** $5M / 0.5062 = $9.88M
5.  **Pre-Money Valuation:** $9.88M - $5M = $4.88M

## 5. The First Chicago Method: Multi-Scenario Weighting

This method combines the VC Method with scenario analysis to smooth out extreme outcomes.

**Logic:**
Estimate the valuation under three distinct scenarios and weight them by probability.

1.  **Success (Upside):** High growth, IPO exit. ($P_s$, $V_s$)
2.  **Survival (Likely/Base):** Moderate growth, M&A exit. ($P_l$, $V_l$)
3.  **Failure (Downside):** Liquidation or "Walking Dead". ($P_f$, $V_f$)

**Combined Valuation Formula:**
$$V_{\text{total}} = (P_s \times V_s) + (P_l \times V_l) + (P_f \times V_f)$$

**Engine Requirement:** The engine must ensure that $\sum P_i = 1.0$.

## 6. Real Options in VC Valuation

Real options account for the flexibility management has to respond to new information. This is critical for businesses with negative NPV that have significant "upside optionality".

### 6.1 The Three Core Factors
1.  **Irreversibility:** The cost of the investment cannot be fully recovered if the project is abandoned.
2.  **Uncertainty:** Higher volatility ($\sigma$) increases the value of the option.
3.  **Timing Freedom:** The ability to delay investment allows the firm to wait for the resolution of uncertainty.

### 6.2 Advanced Option Formulas (Black-Scholes Adaptation)
The engine can value an **Option to Expand** using the Black-Scholes model:
$$C = S N(d_1) - X e^{-rt} N(d_2)$$
*   $S$: Present value of future cash flows from expansion.
*   $X$: Cost of expansion (strike price).
*   $\sigma$: Volatility of the project's value.
*   $t$: Time until the expansion decision must be made.

## 7. Down Rounds and Anti-Dilution

A down round occurs when $V_{\text{pre}, \text{Round } n} < V_{\text{post}, \text{Round } n-1}$.

**Engine Logic for Dilution Protection:**
*   **Full Ratchet:** The previous investors' conversion price is lowered to the price of the new round.
*   **Weighted Average (Broad-Based):**
    $$CP_{new} = CP_{old} \times \frac{S_{old} + \frac{I}{CP_{old}}}{S_{old} + S_{new}}$$
    Where $S_{old}$ is shares outstanding and $S_{new}$ is new shares issued.

## 8. Technical Appendix: Statistical Logic

### 8.1 Scenario Math
For any variable $X$ (Cash Flow, Exit Multiple, etc.), the engine should support:
*   **Expected Value:** $E(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P_i X_i$
*   **Variance:** $Var(X) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P_i (X_i - E(X))^2$
*   **Standard Deviation:** $\sigma(X) = \sqrt{Var(X)}$

### 8.2 Covariance and Correlation Logic
To compute the CEQ adjustment, the engine uses:
*   $\text{Cov}(CF, R_m) = \rho \cdot \sigma(CF) \cdot \sigma_m$
*   The **Market Price of Risk ($\lambda$)**: $\lambda = \frac{r_m - r_f}{\sigma_m^2}$
*   Alternative $CEQ$ formulation: $CEQ = E(CF) - \lambda \text{Cov}(CF, R_m)$

### 8.3 Terminal Value Estimation
The engine calculates Continuing Value ($CV$) at Year $N$:
*   **Multiple Method:** $CV = CF_N \times \text{Exit Multiple}$
*   **Perpetuity Growth Method:** $CV = \frac{CF_N \times (1+g)}{r - g}$
